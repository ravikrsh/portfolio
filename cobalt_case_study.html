01 The Problem

A platform that felt like
a chore

COBALT was Cognizant's internal platform for developer self-learning and certification assessment. The goal was right ‚Äî give thousands of developers a way to upskill and get certified at their own pace. But the experience was working against that goal.

Dropout rates were a persistent, known problem. Learners would start modules and not return. They'd attempt certifications without adequate preparation and fail. The platform treated learning like a compliance task ‚Äî dry, linear, with no visible sense of progress or reward for effort.

"How do you make something people are expected to do feel like something they want to do ‚Äî without making it feel trivial?"

I joined as UX design lead representing Cognizant, coordinating with an external vendor who built and maintained the platform. My job was to translate learner needs and business goals into clear UX direction ‚Äî and make sure what got built actually served users.

03 The Design

Gamification that

The core design principle: every gamification element had to map to a real motivation gap ‚Äî not just make the platform look playful. Badges that reward trivial actions erode trust. Rewards had to feel earned.

01
Intent over instruction

Wireframes were annotated with why each decision mattered ‚Äî not just what to build. This meant the vendor could make informed decisions when edge cases arose.

02
Push back with rationale

When implementation drifted from the intended experience, I'd push back with a clear user-centred reason rather than a personal preference. That built trust.

03
Make them collaborators

When the vendor understood why a decision mattered, they became genuine partners ‚Äî not just executors. Quality went up significantly as a result.

05 Outcome & Reflection

Results that came from
continuous iteration

The improvements didn't happen overnight. They were the result of staying involved post-launch, reading completion data, and feeding observations back into the design ‚Äî month after month.

  • Making mandatory learning feel voluntary is one of the hardest UX problems. The answer wasn't to disguise the obligation ‚Äî it was to give learners genuine agency within it.
  • Gamification only works when it's tied to real behaviour. Every element had to map to something meaningful in the learning journey.
  • Vendor coordination requires clarity of intent, not just clarity of design. Annotated wireframes matter ‚Äî but so does the relationship.
  • Align stakeholders before designing. When L&D leads and sponsors are bought in early, you're executing a shared vision ‚Äî not defending decisions mid-project.
