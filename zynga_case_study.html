01 The Environment

Designing in a
live game is different

Most UX projects have a clear start and end ‚Äî research, design, ship, measure. Live game design doesn't work that way. The product is always on. Always generating data. Always in need of attention.

At Zynga, I was deputed as a game designer on two live Facebook titles ‚Äî Hidden Chronicles and Hidden Shadows. As one of several designers each owning specific systems, I owned daily engagement hooks and in-game economy design. Every week brought new player data and new design decisions.

03 A Specific Design Decision

The reward
timing problem

Of all the iterative changes made across both games, this one illustrates the data-driven design loop most clearly. It started with a pattern I noticed in the economy and engagement data.

üîç
What the data showed

Rewards ‚Äî coins, power-ups, special items ‚Äî were being distributed too early in the session and progression cycle. Players received their best incentives before they had built up enough investment in the session. After the early reward spike, engagement dropped off sharply. And because players already had what they needed, there was less reason to spend.

Before ‚Äî Front-loaded rewards
After ‚Äî Milestone-paced rewards
Player opens game ‚Üí receives best reward immediately ‚Üí completes one or two actions ‚Üí engagement drops ‚Äî nothing left to work toward ‚Üí session ends early ‚Üí spend low, nothing needed
‚Üí
Player opens game ‚Üí small early reward builds momentum ‚Üí meaningful rewards tied to sustained play milestones ‚Üí player has something to work toward throughout the session ‚Üí session depth increases ‚Üí in-game spend improves

How it got acted on

I raised the pattern during a regular data review ‚Äî not as a vague concern, but as a clear hypothesis with a proposed change. The team aligned, the change was implemented, and in-game spend improved.

The key was framing it as a testable hypothesis rather than an opinion. That made it easy for the team to say yes ‚Äî and gave us a clear way to measure whether it worked.

The design principle behind it

Anticipation is one of the most powerful levers in engagement design. Players need something to work toward ‚Äî not just something to collect. When rewards arrive too early, you remove the very tension that makes the game worth playing.

This principle applies equally to learning platforms, onboarding flows, and any experience where return behaviour matters.

04 Economy Design as UX

The economy is
the experience

In-game economy design in a free-to-play game is fundamentally a UX problem. If the economy feels unfair, players leave. If it's too generous, there's no reason to engage with spend mechanics. The balance is finding the point where progression feels achievable ‚Äî but where spending meaningfully improves the experience.

üìâ
Find the friction points

Used economy data to identify where players were getting stuck or losing interest ‚Äî the moments where the game stopped feeling fair.

üéØ
Layer in emotional signals

Support ticket data revealed the emotional response to economy decisions. Numbers show what ‚Äî support feedback shows how it felt.

üõ°
Change conservatively

Economy changes in live games have unintended knock-on effects. Every change was scoped carefully and monitored closely post-implementation.

‚ö†Ô∏è
Live games demand humility

You'll design something that seems right and find out it isn't. The skill isn't avoiding those moments ‚Äî it's building a workflow that surfaces them quickly and lets you respond without ego. Working on live products every week taught me to hold design decisions lightly and trust the data over my own assumptions.

05 What This Taught Me About UX

Four lessons that
still apply everywhere

These insights came from live game design ‚Äî but I've applied every one of them to enterprise UX, learning platforms, and service design since.

01
Data is a design input, not a design answer

The reward timing insight came from the data ‚Äî but the hypothesis and the solution came from design thinking. Analytics tells you what is happening. It takes a designer to figure out why.

02
Raising an insight is a design skill

Spotting the reward timing pattern and framing it as a testable hypothesis ‚Äî not just a vague concern ‚Äî is what got it acted on. Communication is part of the design process.

03
Anticipation beats instant gratification

Players ‚Äî and users of all kinds ‚Äî need something to work toward. When you remove the tension by rewarding too early, you remove the reason to stay engaged.

04
Economy systems are UX systems

They shape how players feel about the product at every moment. Designing them well requires the same empathy and user-centricity as any interface problem ‚Äî maybe more.
